Introduction

Wildfires frequently demonstrate themselves to be one of the most destructive natural and/or man-made disasters known to man. Due to the inherent danger of wildfires to environment it is obviously of great interest to the public to promote the development of tools that will be able to quantify the likelihood of wildfires occurring and the severity of said fires. At the moment, various organizations, both public and private, have developed tools used to calculate the probability that a wildfire will occur at a present moment in time. To function, these models use real-time conditions such as air temperature, precipitation, and wind behavior. In this experiment, the researcher will attempt to construct a model capable of estimating the number of wildfires that will occur in a given region per given year as opposed to one computing the prospects of one occurring at a moment.

According to the Union of Concerned Scientists increases in air temperature have been shown to negatively correlate with soil moisture which in turn makes the likelihood of a wildfire occurring greater. The organization further states that decreases in precipitation and concurrently soil moisture, can also lead to an increase in wildfire incidents. The United States National Park Service reports also, that lightning strikes are a significant factor determining the prospect of wildfires transpiring. The National Park Service also reported that humans are an overwhelming cause of wildfires, estimated to cause the occurrence of approximately 90% of wildfires throughout the country. The goal of the researcher is to utilize the above-mentioned facts to develop a mathematical model that will provide fire suppression agencies with an estimation of how many wildfires a region will experience within the period of year.  

Engineering Goal:

 The goal of this project was to develop a mathematical function that will be able to predict the number of wildfires that occur and the damage that will occur from a wildfire given the climatological profile of a region.

Based on the regression statistics we can conclude that the formula above can explain approximately 89% of the variation in the number of fires, we can also state that the model is significant as the “Significance F” value has a p-value less than .15.  It appears that the minimum number of wildfires that any region will experience will be between 2911 and 4897 fires. The factor that appears to have the most effect on the number of fires is the temperature in ℃, regression analysis reveals that with every 1℃ increase in temperature there is a corresponding 745-909 increase in the number of wildfires.  Following temperature in ℃, regional annual precipitation in (mL) possesses the second most significant effect on the number of wildfires that arise. Regression analysis indicates that as expected a decrease in precipitation leads to an increase in the yearly number of wildfires. To be precise, for every 1 mL increase in annual precipitation there is an approximately .25625 decrease in the yearly total of wildfires. Following precipitation, the number of visitors was the most influential variable in estimating the number of wildfires that take place in a year. Surprisingly, there exists a negative correlation between the number of visitors of national forests in a region and the number of wildfires that occur in said region. For every 1,000 persons increase in regional national forest attendance, there is a .24917 decrease in wildfires. Lastly, the factor that least affects the yearly total of wildfires is the number of lightning strikes. Also surprising is the fact that the an increase in lightning strikes results in an almost insignificant decrease in the number of fires, with 1 increase in the yearly amount of lightning strikes resulting in an approximately .00537 decrease in the yearly number of wildfires.

Conclusion:

In short, the model produced explained 89% of the variation in the dependent variable, in this case the number of wildfires that occurred in a given year. The variables in order which had the greatest influence on the dependent variable’s value were the temperature in ℃ with an approximately 827 change in the number of lightning strikes per 1 change in ℃. This was followed by regional annual precipitation in mL, with a .25625 change in the total number of wildfires per 1 change in mL of annual precipitation. Following annual precipitation, number of visitors (in 1000s) had the most effect on the number of wildfires that occurred in a year, witnessing a -.24917 change in the number of wildfires per a given year per 1,000 persons positive change in the number of visitors to national forests in a region. Finally, the number of lightning strikes had the least power in affecting the number of wildfires that a region experienced in a given year with there being a -.00537 change in the number of wildfires per 1 increase in the number of lightning strikes that occurred that year. If given the chance to repeat this project the researcher would peruse through sources to find a more accurate alternative than the one used to find the state annual precipitation rate, because the information in the Climatological Data Publications only list the average annual precipitation for a district instead of the total annual precipitation districts that might have a higher total annual precipitation than those of others are not receiving the proper weight that they should. In addition to the concerns addressed above, another point of concern for the researcher is the fact that there was no documents that could be found that would provide the researcher with yearly instead of periodic values for the visitor attendance information, perhaps by doing this future research attempts will be able to resolve the issue of having a constant visitor attendance value for the concerned region.
